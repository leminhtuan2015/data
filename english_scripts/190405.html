A self-proclaimed white supremacist accused of last month's deadly mass shootings at two New Zealand mosques is to be kept in custody until June 14.

Twenty-eight-year-old Australian Brenton Harrison Tarrant appeared in court via video link at the High Court in Christchurch Friday morning local time.

The judge ordered a mental health assessment of the suspect who appeared from Auckland, New Zealand, where he is in custody.

Fifty people died and many more were injured March 15 when a gunman opened fire at two mosques in Christchurch.

Tarrant was expected to be charged with 50 counts of murder and 39 counts of attempted murder.



The first official report on the crash of an Ethiopian jet says the crew followed the proper procedures.

Reuters Mia Womersley reports.

A preliminary report into the Ethiopian Airlines crash concludes that the plane repeatedly nose dived before it crashed and has called on Boeing to review the control system of its 737 Max 8 aircraft.

"It is recommended that the aircraft flight control system relative to the flight controllability shall be reviewed by the manufacturer."

At a briefing on Thursday, the Ethiopian minister of transport said the jet was in good condition and airworthy, adding that Flight 302 took off normally at first.

157 passengers and crew of 37 nationalities were killed.

That's Reuters Mia Womersley reporting.



NATO foreign ministers met in Washington Thursday to celebrate the alliance's [7th] 70th anniversary.

U.S. Secretary of State Mike Pompeo opened the meeting with an appeal for unity to confront great power challenges from Russia, China and Iran.

After the meeting, NATO Secretary-General Jens Stoltenberg said the members agreed on action to confront "a more assertive Russia."

President Trump is floating new ideas for stopping the flow of illegal drugs at the U.S. border with Mexico - put tariffs on Mexican-made cars.

AP's Sagar Meghani reports.

Last week, the president threatened to close the southern border if Mexico did not immediately stop all illegal immigration into the U.S. and later praised the nation for stepping up.

Today, "So we need help from Mexico. If Mexico doesn't give the help, that's OK," saying he might then put tariffs on cars coming into the U.S. from Mexico, calling it "a very powerful incentive." "I will do it."

He is also threatening tariffs if Mexico does not stop the illegal drug flow in a year.

Sagar Meghani, at the White House.



Ugandan security forces are still searching for an American tourist and a local driver who were abducted at gunpoint this week in a national park.

Uganda Police Spokeswoman [Polly ...], pardon me, Polly Namaye: "The Police has dispatched an elite squad from the Tourism Police, to reinforce the security teams at Queen Elizabeth National Park, and actively pursue, a group of four unknown gunmen who allegedly kidnapped an American and a Ugandan senior guide who were on an evening game drive."

Ugandan police have identified the American as Kimberly Sue Endecott and her driver as Jean Paul.

Uganda says the kidnappers used the victim's phone to demand a ransom of $500,000.



Pope Francis has named Archbishop Wilton Gregory as the new head of the archdiocese of Washington.

The 71-year-old Gregory will become the first African-American to lead the Church's most influential archdiocese in the United States.

Gregory says he will strife to regain the trust of a congregation that became the epicenter of the clergy sex abuse crisis in the United States.

"... together we will not merely address the moments where we've fallen short or failed outright, but we will model for all the life and teaching of our Lord Jesus Christ and we will reclaim the future."



President Trump met today at the White House with Chinese Vice Premier Liu He, a sign that the world's two top economic powers could be nearing a trade agreement after marathon talks.

The two countries had originally hoped to reach an agreement by March 1, but even as the negotiations have extended, Mr. Trump and his economic advisers say the discussions are progressing. Some officials have suggested a deal could be reached by the end of April.



President Trump is expected to veto a resolution passed by the U.S. House of Representatives today meant to end participation in the civil war in Yemen.

House members voted mostly along party lines in what could be seen as a rejection of the Trump administration's support for the Saudi-led military campaign.
