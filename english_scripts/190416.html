A major fire has broken out at the famed Notre Dame Cathedral in central Paris, causing huge damage to the 12th century building.

AP correspondent Angela Charlton reports the French president, Emmanuel Macron, summed up people's feelings by saying the fire consuming the cathedral is like a part of us being on fire.

It's unclear what happening inside the building. It's a huge cathedral. There are a lot of relics and other important artworks inside. Officials say they're working to protect those artworks but it's unclear if anything has already been damaged.

It is also not clear what caused the fire or if anyone has been hurt. But firefighters now say they have saved the building's two iconic towers.

Demonstrators in Sudan's capital are defying attempts by the military to break up their ongoing protest.

Correspondent Naba Mohiedeen reports for VOA from Khartoum.

Protesters on Monday said troops tried to clear their ongoing sit-in outside army headquarters in Khartoum. But the protesters who have camped out since the military announced the removal of longtime President Omar al-Bashir last Thursday refused to move.

The protesters are demanding a quicker move to civilian rule in Sudan than the military's announced two-year transition to an elected government.

The military's transitional leadership has changed twice since it said Bashir had been placed under house arrest following four months of popular demonstrations.

An independent monitoring group in Pakistan is saying it's concerned about the state of human rights in the country. It released a report on Monday.

VOA correspondent Ayesha Tanzeem reports from Islamabad.

The Human Rights Commission of Pakistan expressed concern over what it said were massive violations of human rights in Pakistan last year.

The HRCP secretary-general, Harris Khalique, said the report offered an opportunity to focus on the real issues.



More than 30 journalists and publishers faced charges Monday over the reporting in Australia on Cardinal George Pell's trial for child sex abuse. Prosecutors are seeking fines and jail terms over accusations that the news outlets breached strict suppression orders in the case. Experts, however, say the reporting ban raises issues about freedom of the press.

Correspondent Phil Mercer reports for VOA from Sydney.

Several Australian agents say they are accused of breaching the ban by reporting cryptically that an unnamed high-profile person had been found guilty of a serious crime that couldn't be made public.

Experts say Australian reporting restrictions were futile in the global digital age.

Some of Australia's biggest media companies are among those facing contempt allegations. Most intend to vigorously fight the charges.
