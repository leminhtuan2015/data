French President Emmanuel Macron has pledged to cunt income taxes for middle class workers.

Macron made the vow Thursday during a planned speech outlining his response to months of anti-government protests.

He said it's up to the government to decide the tax rate. It seems to me that if we manage to reduce the income tax by five billion euros more than what has already been done and planned, it will be a significant gesture.

The French president said he wants to cut taxes for a maximum number of citizens and especially those who are working, the middle class.

Macron said he is considering financing the measure through cuts in public spending and by making people work longer before they retire. He didn't elaborate on that last point.

The speech comes after nearly six months of so-called "yellow vest" protests over Macron's economic policies.

Tens of thousands of people marched in Sudan's capital Thursday for what organizers called a "million strong march" in support of civilian rule just weeks after Omar al-Bashir was ousted from power.

Naba Mohiedeen reports from Khartoum.

Tens of thousands of Sudanese poured onto Khartoum's streets Thursday, calling for military leaders who toppled Omar al-Bashir to hand over power to civilian leaders.

Sudan's main protest group, the Sudanese Professionals Association, organized the march after talks with the Transitional Military Council deadlocked.

Three members of the TMC resigned late Wednesday after the SPA called for the march.

The military ousted Bashir on April 11 after three decades in power, and for months of anti-government protests, military leaders took over, promising elections within two years. But protesters are demanding immediate civilian rule and have refused to leave the streets.

Naba Mohiedeen, for VOA news, Khartoum.



The human rights group, Amnesty International, says U.S. coalition forces killed more than 1,600 civilians in an air campaign to retake the city of Raqqa from the Islamic State.

As Henry Ridgwell reports, the figure is many times more than that acknowledged by the coalition.

Following a two-year investigation, the group says it has gathered names for more than one thousand of the victims and says it has verified 641 deaths on the ground in Raqqa.

"This is not a situation of a few individual cases, isolated cases. It is much more systemic than that."

Amnesty worked alongside the investigative group Airwars spending two months on the ground in Raqqa, interviewing survivors and visiting strike sites.

In response to the evidence it has presented, Amnesty says coalition forces have acknowledged far fewer civilian deaths.

Henry Ridgwell, for VOA news, London.



The U.S. Embassy in Colombo has advised people to avoid places of worship in Sri Lanka this weekend, citing Sri Lankan reports that additional attacks could occur.

The warning comes just days after a devastating attack on Christian worshippers Easter Sunday when suicide bombers killed more than 250 people. Officials had earlier set the death toll at more than 350 but revised the number Thursday, saying some of the bodies may have been counted twice.

Sri Lankan police continue making arrests in connection with Sunday's attacks, telling reporters that they have arrested more than 70 men and women from all over the country.
