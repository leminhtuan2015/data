Boeing and the Federal Aviation Administration say more time is needed for a software fix for the troubled 737 Max airliners.

AP correspondent Mike Gracia reports.

Boeing and U.S. aviation administrators are indicating more time will be needed to finish changes to a flight control system on the 737 Max airliners.

Boeing was expected to complete the work last week but an FAA spokesman said Monday the company needs more time to make sure all issues were identified and addressed.

The MAX aircraft were grounded around the world in mid-March following the March 10 crash of an Ethiopian Airlines MAX8 jet shortly after takeoff.

In addition to approval from the FAA, once changes are made, Boeing will also need clearance from safety officials in Europe and China.

A Democratic-controlled congressional panel on Tuesday subpoenaed a former White House security clearance chief to answer questions about a whistleblower's claims that senior Trump administration officials granted national security clearances to at least 25 people that government reviewers had rejected for an array of reasons.

The House Oversight and Reform Committee approved the subpoena to Carl Kline on a party-line 22-15 vote after the committee's top Republican, Congressman Jim Jordan, protested, calling the investigation a "partisan attack" and an "excuse to go fishing" through personnel files of White House officials.

But Democrats led by the panel's chairman, Elijah Cummings, moved ahead with the investigation based on claims by a government security clearance official under both Republican and Democratic presidents.
